## arXiv Independence (March 2026)

arXiv is becoming an independent nonprofit (July 1, 2026) after 35 years under
Cornell. Simons Foundation backing, hiring a CEO, $6M budget.

Angle: What does it mean when the dominant preprint server becomes a standalone
nonprofit? Not a Press pitch — an honest analysis of governance, sustainability,
and what researchers should watch for. Written from the perspective of a
researcher who builds infrastructure.

Questions to explore:
- Does independence let arXiv evolve faster, or does nonprofit governance add its
  own inertia?
- What should researchers watch for in the CEO hire? (Tech-forward vs.
  governance-focused signals very different futures.)
- The community's OpenAI anxiety — is it warranted, or is arXiv's incentive
  structure fundamentally different?
- What does arXiv's $6M/27-staff reality tell us about the cost of maintaining
  scholarly infrastructure?
- Will an independent arXiv ever expand beyond PDF?

## "Claude Scholar" and the Automating-Away-the-Researcher Pipeline

Open-source tool called "Claude Scholar" (João Cláudio Nunes Carvalho, LinkedIn
2026-03-29) claims to automate 90% of the academic research lifecycle:
literature review, statistical analysis, paper writing, citation verification,
"language refinement to sound human," quality self-review, and rebuttal
drafting. Targets NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, ACL, AAAI, Nature, Science, Cell, PNAS.

Angle: This is the logical endpoint of the AI-paper-mill trajectory, now
packaged as a product and marketed on LinkedIn with engagement-bait framing
("Professors are going to hate this"). Not a competitor to Aris — it's the
environment Press operates in. Worth covering because:

- **The "sound human" feature is the tell.** A tool that explicitly includes
  "language refinement to sound human" as a feature is acknowledging that AI
  writing doesn't sound human — and that the goal is to hide the provenance.
  This is the opposite of transparency.

- **The framing is adversarial to academia.** "Professors are going to hate
  this" positions the tool as disrupting the reviewer/author relationship, not
  improving it. The attitude maps to the broader anti-institutional sentiment
  in AI-for-science discourse.

- **This is what peer review is now receiving.** If ICLR 2026 already had 21%
  AI-generated reviews, tools like this close the loop: AI writes the paper,
  AI reviews the paper, AI writes the rebuttal. The human is the one paying
  the API bill.

- **Connects to aiXiv.** aiXiv accepts AI-authored papers. Claude Scholar
  generates them. The pipeline is assembling itself. What does "research" mean
  when the tools for producing, reviewing, and hosting papers are all
  automated?

Questions to explore:
- Is there a meaningful distinction between "AI-assisted writing" and "AI
  writing with a human signature"?
- What happens to peer review when reviewers can't distinguish AI-generated
  submissions from human-written ones?
- Does open-sourcing this kind of tool accelerate the problem or make it more
  visible (and therefore more addressable)?

Sources:
- https://www.linkedin.com/posts/joaoclaudionc_professors-are-going-to-hate-this-someone-share-7443752038334812163-3uz3

## The Answer to AI Slop Isn't Better Detectors — It's Better Formats

The Atlantic's "Science Is Drowning in AI Slop" (Jan 2026) + Richard Sever's
Newton Institute thread (Apr 2026) frame a clean angle: the entire AI slop
discourse treats this as a detection problem (build better AI catchers). The
structural argument is that opaque document formats (PDF) make fraud easy and
verification hard. Semantic, machine-readable formats (RSM) make provenance,
reproducibility, and accountability legible by design.

Sever separately flags the "author-facing vs post-hoc verification" tension —
should AI checking happen during writing or after publication? Aris bets on
author-facing (Press/Studio integrate verification into the workflow).

Angle: Not a product pitch. An honest argument that the format layer is upstream
of every detection tool, and that the community is solving the wrong problem.
