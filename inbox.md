- [Artificial intelligence tools expand scientists' impact but contract science's focus](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09922-y)
  Hao et al. (Nature 649, 1237, 2026). LEAD CANDIDATE: 41.3 million research papers across the natural sciences analyzed using a pretrained language model to identify AI-augmented work. Scientists who engaged in AI-augmented research publish 3.02x more papers and receive 4.84x more citations than non-users, but AI use is linked to a 5% reduction in topic range and a 22% drop in collaboration. The paradox: individual scientific impact expands while collective scientific reach contracts, as AI-augmented work moves toward areas richest in data. The hardest empirical evidence yet for the homogenization-with-productivity-gain thesis. arXiv preprint: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.07727. Pair with: CHBAH on LLM-assisted writing (edition 012), Tang and Yang on AI agents narrowing exploration (edition 010), social-consequences-of-AI-delegation framing (edition 012), and the "Will AI ruin the social sciences" piece (edition 013).

  Tracy Teal for openRxiv (June 26 2026): bioRxiv now offers Reviewer3 as an opt-in "External Author Service" providing comprehensive technical review within minutes. - [Rethinking bioinformatics expertise in the era of artificial intelligence](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-026-02777-1)
  Goh et al. (npj Digital Medicine vol 9, article 398, 2026): perspective arguing AI is an accelerant whose value depends on expert oversight at the points where training data, architecture, and deployment context are defined. Concrete case: a commercial algorithm used to identify patients for additional care management systematically underestimated Black patients' needs because it used healthcare costs as a proxy for health need, encoding existing disparities into the model. The point Luciano Martelotto pulled from it on LinkedIn: "the model has no idea when it is producing nonsense, and that, that IS the whole problem" — AI cannot validate biological plausibility, detect batch effects, or spot label noise; domain expertise remains irreplaceable for quality control. Replaces a LinkedIn post by Luciano Martelotto. Pairs with the NEJM AI/Boston Children's case in edition 013 (capability WITH expert oversight, the 4.8% yield came from cases experts had already analyzed) and the Hao et al. result above (individual capability gains, collective focus contraction): both rest on whether expert judgment stays in the loop.

- [AI Scientists as Engines of Discovery: A Case for Development within Reformed Institutions](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22859)
  Jimenez, Bolliet, Villaescusa-Navarro, Zbib, Wandelt, Spergel, Meier, Montgomery, Aliee, Verde (arXiv:2606.22859, June 22 2026). Heavy cosmology and AI lineup (Spergel, Verde, Villaescusa-Navarro, Wandelt) arguing agentic multi-agent AI systems are a qualitative rather than incremental shift, capable of evolving from computational tools into autonomous "AI scientists" with their own role in the discovery cycle. Demonstrates with a prototype framework called Denario. Calls for institutional reform around verification, accountability, interpretability, and dual-use safety, framing AI as "an epistemic actor rather than a mere instrument." The move is the framing: senior cosmologists explicitly proposing AI agents as epistemic actors that need governance distinct from tool-use. Pairs with Hao et al. above (the empirical) and the Wilson editorial (the incentives) for a three-angle take on AI's role in the research lifecycle.

- [Towards End-to-End Automation of AI Research](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.15497)
  Yamada, Lange, Cong Lu, Chris Lu, Hu, Foerster, Ha, Clune (arXiv:2606.15497, submitted March 31 2026, processed June 2026). Sakana AI team's latest "AI Scientist" system, generating research ideas, code, experiments, manuscripts, AND its own peer review. The headline result: an AI-generated manuscript passes the first round of peer review at a major ML conference workshop (workshop acceptance rate 70%). Two modes: template-guided focused research, and template-free agentic open-ended exploration. The authors themselves name the risks: "taxing overwhelmed review systems and adding noise to scientific literature." Pairs with Jimenez et al. above as the demonstrated-capability sibling to the institutional-reform argument. Triangulates with Hao et al. (the empirical, productivity-with-focus-contraction) and Jimenez et al. (the institutional warning) for a complete view of the AI-as-research-agent thread: it ships, it works at conference-workshop quality, and the only governance framework around it is what the field decides to build.

- [Advancing Mathematics Research with AI-Driven Formal Proof Search](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22763)
  Tsoukalas et al. with Adam Zsolt Wagner, Eric Wieser, Aja Huang, Pushmeet Kohli, Thomas Hubert, Matej Balog, Henryk Michalewski (DeepMind plus Lean community plus mathematicians, arXiv:2605.22763, submitted May 21 2026, v2 June 8 2026). First large-scale evaluation of LLM-plus-Lean formal-proof-search agents on open math problems. The most capable agent autonomously resolved 9 of 353 open Erdős problems at a per-problem cost of a few hundred dollars, proved 44 of 492 OEIS conjectures, and is being deployed by researchers in combinatorics, optimization, graph theory, algebraic geometry, and quantum optics. A basic agent (LLM-generation alternating with Lean-verification) replicated the Erdős successes but cost more on the hardest problems. The math-and-AI-in-science thread the inbox has been quiet on. Where Yamada et al. (Sakana, above) demonstrates end-to-end AI authorship at ML conference quality, Tsoukalas et al. demonstrates AI-aided proof search at open-mathematics quality, with verification anchored in Lean's type-theoretic kernel rather than in human peer review.
