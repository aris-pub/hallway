- [Artificial intelligence tools expand scientists' impact but contract science's focus](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09922-y)
  Hao et al. (Nature 649, 1237, 2026). LEAD CANDIDATE: 41.3 million research papers across the natural sciences analyzed using a pretrained language model to identify AI-augmented work. Scientists who engaged in AI-augmented research publish 3.02x more papers and receive 4.84x more citations than non-users, but AI use is linked to a 5% reduction in topic range and a 22% drop in collaboration. The paradox: individual scientific impact expands while collective scientific reach contracts, as AI-augmented work moves toward areas richest in data. The hardest empirical evidence yet for the homogenization-with-productivity-gain thesis. arXiv preprint: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.07727. Pair with: CHBAH on LLM-assisted writing (edition 012), Tang and Yang on AI agents narrowing exploration (edition 010), social-consequences-of-AI-delegation framing (edition 012), and the "Will AI ruin the social sciences" piece (edition 013).

- [Rethinking bioinformatics expertise in the era of artificial intelligence](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-026-02777-1)
  Goh et al. (npj Digital Medicine vol 9, article 398, 2026): perspective arguing AI is an accelerant whose value depends on expert oversight at the points where training data, architecture, and deployment context are defined. Concrete case: a commercial algorithm used to identify patients for additional care management systematically underestimated Black patients' needs because it used healthcare costs as a proxy for health need, encoding existing disparities into the model. The point Luciano Martelotto pulled from it on LinkedIn: "the model has no idea when it is producing nonsense, and that, that IS the whole problem" — AI cannot validate biological plausibility, detect batch effects, or spot label noise; domain expertise remains irreplaceable for quality control. Replaces a LinkedIn post by Luciano Martelotto. Pairs with the NEJM AI/Boston Children's case in edition 013 (capability WITH expert oversight, the 4.8% yield came from cases experts had already analyzed) and the Hao et al. result above (individual capability gains, collective focus contraction): both rest on whether expert judgment stays in the loop.

- [Will AI spark a scientific renaissance, or a diffuse monoculture?](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01954-2)
  Suzi Wilson (Nature 654, 843, June 22 2026): editorial pairing with the Hao et al. study above, arguing that AI's effect on science depends less on model capability than on whether researchers, reviewers, and funders reward originality over speed. The institutional framing of the empirical result. Connects to the incentive critique Leo articulated in response to Di Matteo's NetSci 2026 panel: academia rewards papers in high-impact journals, not creativity or responsibility.
