- [Peer Reviewers Should be Allowed to Upload Manuscripts to AI](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/410696217_Peer_Reviewers_Should_be_Allowed_to_Upload_Manuscripts_to_AI)
  ResearchGate publication 410696217, July 2026 (agent should WebFetch or search for exact author list, venue, and date; the ResearchGate abstract page was not accessible during this inbox add). The title itself is the position: contrarian to the current publisher consensus (Wiley, Elsevier, JAMA all prohibit reviewer AI upload on confidentiality grounds; per 2024 review, 93% of medical journals that gave guidance prohibited it). Fits the AI-in-peer-review thread directly: pairs with the Frontiers Director of Research Integrity piece "Now is the Time for AI in Peer Review, and Publishing Policies Need to Recognize This" (edition 015), the Vancouver Standard consultation (edition 017), Reviewer3 on bioRxiv (edition 013), NeurIPS hidden prompts (edition 015), and the ICML violations data (edition 015 mentions 506 reviewers caught, 497 desk-rejections). Also lands close to Thorp's Science editorial (edition 017 lead): where Thorp says AI makes review slower/worse/more expensive, this paper argues the opposite — restricting reviewer AI use is the cost. Worth framing the two together as the field arguing the same policy question from opposite sides.

- [Networked Intelligence: Active Shared Context Graphs for Human-AI Team Science](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13220)
  Choudhury, Czajka, Monteiro, Bredeweg, McDermott, Wolf, Beliaev et al. (author list reads as Pacific Northwest National Lab, arXiv July 14 2026, revised July 16). Argues the AI-for-science thread edition 014-017 has been tracking — Biomni, Co-Scientist, Robin, Sakana End-to-End, Denario — scales INDIVIDUAL AI reasoners, while real science is team work with diverse expertise. Proposes framing networked intelligence as "sparse conditional computation over distributed scientific contexts" and clarifying when standalone scaled agents suffice versus when networked approaches become essential. Introduces **Mycelium**, an active shared workspace that automatically connects researchers and AI agents, captures observations and hypotheses, tracks them against evolving knowledge, and routes insights to relevant team members or agents. Tested on a biological multi-omics campaign: shared context turned a localized analytical finding into cross-disciplinary mechanistic constraints and influenced experimental design. Direct methodological counterpoint to the solo-AI-scientist wave of the last three editions. Also formalizes Thorp's July 9 editorial thesis from edition 017 lead ("AI... requires more human effort, not less"): the paper's answer is that the additional human effort should be team-embedded, not just verification-embedded.

- [Challengescape: a living landscape of AI-for-science challenges](https://encode-challengescape.pillar.vc/)
  Encode program (Pillar VC, powered by ARIA, funded by UK Government), publicly surfaced via Georgia Channing (Hugging Face) on LinkedIn July 2026. Directory of **120+ real AI-for-science problems** submitted by experts (mostly professors) with mentor support from established labs, so researchers without years of domain-specific taste can pick up worthwhile work. Sits at the meta-organizational layer of the AI-in-science thread this newsletter has been tracking: instead of shipping another AI-scientist tool (Biomni, Claude Science, Robin) or building the substrate under one (OXA, ARCH, Crossref Knowledge Graph), Challengescape organizes the problem space itself and routes contributors to it. Notable that ARIA (the UK's Advanced Research and Invention Agency) is powering it: this is state-backed AI-for-science coordination, not a vendor product or an academic-lab initiative. Pairs with the ecosystem-level moves in field-signals (ICOR / NASEM Action Collaborative for institutional reform, Continuous Science Foundation for standards) as a third coordination layer: problems and matchmaking rather than governance or format.
