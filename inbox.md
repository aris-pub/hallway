- [The Last Human-Written Paper: Agent-Native Research Artifacts](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24658)
  Liu et al. (Stanford + CMU + collaborators, arXiv April 27 2026, v3 May 19): proposes replacing traditional narrative papers with Agent-Native Research Artifacts (ARAs), a machine-executable framework with four layers (scientific logic, executable code, exploration graph of failed attempts, evidence grounding). - [Attribution, Provenance, Reference, Citation, and AI for Research Applications: Understanding the Differences](https://scholarlykitchen.sspnet.org/2026/06/17/attribution-provenance-reference-citation-and-ai-for-research-applications-understanding-the-differences/)
  Todd A Carpenter (Scholarly Kitchen / NISO, June 17 2026). Vocabulary precision argument: as AI systems incorporate source materials into outputs, the scholarly community needs to distinguish attribution (work ascribed to author), provenance (origin history and pedigree), citation (formal reference with location details), and reference (broader source documentation). Generative systems operate backwards: generate claims, then apply citations, vs. human research where citation drives the claim. Without deterministic connections to original sources, AI breaks "the value chains and trust that underpin the scholarly communication ecosystem." Names NISO, COUNTER, Cambridge University Press, Center for Open Science, SSP as stakeholders. Pairs directly with Toler/Scholarly Kitchen interview (edition 012) on chain of custody — Toler named the infrastructure gap, Carpenter sharpens the vocabulary needed to fix it. Also pairs with the Liu et al. ARA paper above: ARA is the producer-side answer; Carpenter's distinctions are the metadata layer needed to make ARAs interoperable.
