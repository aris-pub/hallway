Two large infrastructure bets landed in the same week for academic AI research: $152M from NSF for open compute (model weights, training data, full artifacts), and $20M from Biohub/Wellcome to sustain the open-source libraries that compute depends on.

Meanwhile, Google DeepMind published a stateful AI workbench for mathematics that helped solve an open problem, and a bioRxiv preprint showed a compiler-verified system converting published protocols into executable lab code at 95% fidelity.

The pattern: AI tools for research are shifting from question-answering to session-maintaining. The infrastructure question is whether academic researchers get open access to that capability or rent it.
