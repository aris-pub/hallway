This week the "AI scientist" stopped being a thought experiment.

A Sakana AI system generated a paper, including its own peer review, and cleared the first round at an ML conference workshop. A DeepMind-and-Lean proof-search agent autonomously resolved 9 open Erdős problems at a few hundred dollars each. Senior cosmologists published a case for treating AI as an "epistemic actor" rather than an instrument, and called for institutional reform to match.

Capability is no longer the open question. The harder one is institutional. A Nature analysis of 41.3 million papers found that AI-augmented researchers publish 3x more and earn nearly 5x the citations, while their topic range narrows and collaboration drops 22%. Individual impact expands; collective reach contracts.

The line researchers are now drawing in practice is about where human judgment retains authority: bioRxiv's new Reviewer3 service hands technical review to AI but keeps importance assessment with people.
